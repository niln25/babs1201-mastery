Hard

A cell with 2n=4 enters meiosis. At metaphase II of meiosis, how many chromosomes, chromatids, and DNA molecules are present in each cell?

  • A) 4 chromosomes, 4 chromatids, 4 DNA molecules
  • B) 2 chromosomes, 4 chromatids (2 sister chromatid pairs), 4 DNA molecules per cell
  • C) 4 chromosomes, 8 chromatids, 8 DNA molecules
  • D) 2 chromosomes, 2 chromatids, 2 DNA molecules
Worked Solution

Answer: B — At metaphase II: meiosis I has already separated homologs. Each cell is haploid (n=2 for 2n=4 organism). Each chromosome still consists of 2 sister chromatids joined at the centromere (they were not separated in meiosis I). So: 2 chromosomes (each is a pair of sister chromatids) = 4 chromatids = 4 DNA molecules (one per chromatid). DNA content = 2C. Meiosis II will separate these sister chromatids → each of the 4 resulting cells: 2 chromosomes, 2 chromatids (one each), 2 DNA molecules = 1C.

Hard

Rb (retinoblastoma protein) is a tumour suppressor that inhibits E2F transcription factors. When growth factors stimulate a cell, cyclin D levels rise and CDK4/6 become active. Trace the molecular pathway from growth factor binding to DNA synthesis initiation.

  • A) Growth factor → Rb activation → E2F release → S phase genes repressed → DNA synthesis stopped
  • B) Growth factor → receptor tyrosine kinase activation → Ras → MAPK → cyclin D expression ↑ → cyclin D-CDK4/6 formed → partially phosphorylates Rb → cyclin E-CDK2 formed → hyperphosphorylates Rb → Rb releases E2F → E2F activates S phase genes (cyclin E, CDC6, ORC) → DNA replication initiation
  • C) Growth factor → p53 activation → p21 induction → CDK4/6 activation → S phase entry
  • D) Growth factor → direct activation of DNA polymerase → S phase without signalling cascade
Worked Solution

Answer: B — The G1/S restriction point: In G1, Rb is hypophosphorylated and bound to E2F transcription factors, repressing S phase genes. Growth factors activate MAPK → AP-1 → cyclin D transcription. Cyclin D-CDK4/6 complex partially phosphorylates Rb → partial de-repression → E2F partially released → cyclin E transcription. Cyclin E-CDK2 hyperphosphorylates Rb in a positive feedback loop → complete Rb dissociation from E2F → E2F fully active → transcribes: cyclin E, DHFR (nucleotide synthesis), CDC25A, CDC6, PCNA, etc. → S phase entry. This is a bistable switch (the Rb-E2F bistability ensures the G1/S transition is irreversible). Rb is the "guardian of the restriction point" — mutations in both alleles are required to bypass it (Knudson's two-hit hypothesis — hence retinoblastoma requires both RB1 allele losses).

Hard

A cancer cell has all of the following mutations: (1) constitutively active Ras (oncogene), (2) loss of p53, (3) loss of Rb. Why would the combination of all three be more transforming than any single mutation alone?

  • A) Multiple mutations are required simply because cells have redundant mechanisms to repair single mutations
  • B) Each mutation removes a different barrier: (1) constitutively active Ras provides continuous growth signal independent of external factors; (2) Rb loss allows S phase entry regardless of growth signals (bypasses G1 checkpoint); (3) p53 loss prevents cells from arresting or undergoing apoptosis in response to DNA damage or oncogene stress. Together they create unrestrained proliferation (Ras), unconditional cell cycle entry (Rb-), and inability to activate anti-proliferative failsafes (p53-). Each single mutation triggers the other fail-safes
  • C) All three are needed because they encode subunits of the same protein complex
  • D) Cancer requires exactly three mutations — fewer are insufficient regardless of which genes
Worked Solution

Answer: B — Multi-hit carcinogenesis: normal cells have redundant failsafes. (1) Mutant Ras alone: sends growth signal → but this is "oncogene stress" → activates p53 → senescence or apoptosis. So active Ras CANNOT alone transform a cell with intact p53. (2) Rb loss alone: cells enter S phase without growth signals → but DNA errors → p53 → apoptosis. (3) p53 loss alone: allows accumulation of mutations → but growth signals still needed (intact Ras) and cell cycle still gated (intact Rb). All three together: active Ras provides continuous signal AND p53 loss prevents oncogenic stress response AND Rb loss allows cell cycle entry → transformation. This illustrates Hanahan and Weinberg's hallmarks of cancer concept: multiple independent barriers must be overcome. Classic Weinberg lab experiment: transformation requires Ras + E1A (Rb inactivation) + E1B (p53 inactivation) = all three cooperate to fully transform primary rodent cells.

Hard

Meiotic non-disjunction can occur in meiosis I or meiosis II. Down syndrome (trisomy 21) from non-disjunction in meiosis I versus meiosis II would produce different patterns of heterozygosity for chromosome 21 markers. How do they differ?

  • A) There is no difference — both produce identical trisomy 21 offspring
  • B) Meiosis I non-disjunction: both homologs fail to separate → one gamete receives BOTH chromosome 21 homologs (one maternal, one paternal → heterozygous for chr 21 markers); meiosis II non-disjunction: homologs separate normally but sister chromatids fail to separate in meiosis II → one gamete receives 2 copies of the SAME chromosome 21 (homozygous for chr 21 markers). DNA fingerprinting of parent and child can distinguish the two origins
  • C) Meiosis I non-disjunction occurs only in females; meiosis II only in males
  • D) Meiosis II non-disjunction produces more severe Down syndrome than meiosis I
Worked Solution

Answer: B — This question tests understanding of meiosis I vs II division logic combined with genetics. Meiosis I: homologs separate. Non-disjunction: both homologs go to the same pole. The resulting disomic gamete has one maternal chromosome 21 + one paternal chromosome 21 → they will be heterozygous for any polymorphic marker on chromosome 21 (different alleles from mom vs dad). Meiosis II: sister chromatids separate. Non-disjunction: sister chromatids of the same homolog go to the same pole. The resulting disomic gamete has two copies of the SAME chromosome (identical sequences) → homozygous for chromosome 21 markers. Molecular analysis of short tandem repeats (STRs) on chromosome 21 using DNA from the trisomic child and both parents can determine: (1) which parent contributed the extra chromosome, and (2) whether non-disjunction occurred in meiosis I or II. ~95% of Down syndrome cases are due to meiosis I non-disjunction in the mother, increasing with maternal age (spindle checkpoint becomes less stringent with age).

Hard

Colchicine is a drug that binds tubulin monomers and prevents their polymerisation into microtubules. Why would colchicine treatment produce cells with highly variable chromosome numbers (aneuploidy) rather than simply stopping mitosis?

  • A) Colchicine causes random DNA double-strand breaks that generate chromosome fragments
  • B) Colchicine depolymerises the mitotic spindle → SAC activation → cells arrest in metaphase initially. With prolonged treatment, cells can "slip" out of mitotic arrest (mitotic slippage) — APC/C slowly degrades cyclin B even with active SAC → cells exit mitosis without dividing; chromosomes decondense into a single tetraploid nucleus (4N). If such cells re-enter the cell cycle, they have doubled ploidy. Alternatively, with incomplete spindle disruption, some chromosomes may attach to only one pole → non-disjunction → aneuploidy upon eventual division
  • C) Colchicine inhibits cytokinesis specifically, allowing nuclear division without cell division
  • D) Colchicine causes premature sister chromatid separation in all cells
Worked Solution

Answer: B — Colchicine is used clinically (gout, FMF) and experimentally. In biology: it's used to create polyploid plants (colchiploids) for crop improvement. Mechanism of aneuploidy: prolonged mitotic arrest → mitotic slippage (slow APC/C activity despite spindle checkpoint) → cells exit with undivided chromosomes → tetraploidy. Subsequent division attempts of tetraploid cells are highly error-prone (multiple centrosomes → multipolar spindles → chromosomes attached to wrong poles → non-disjunction → random aneuploidy). Additionally, at sub-optimal colchicine doses: some microtubules remain → partial spindles → some chromosomes mismatch → aneuploidy. This is why colchicine at appropriate doses is used to arrest cells in metaphase for karyotyping (chromosomes condensed and spread after colchicine arrest + hypotonic treatment + fixation = metaphase spread for chromosome analysis).

Hard

A researcher knocks out the APC/C activator Cdh1 (which targets cyclin B and securin for degradation in late mitosis). What would happen to cell cycle progression?

  • A) Cells would permanently arrest in G1 because Cdh1-APC/C keeps cells in G1
  • B) Cells would initially enter mitosis normally (Cdc20-APC/C handles anaphase onset), but exit from mitosis would be impaired — cyclin B would not be degraded efficiently, maintaining CDK1 activity beyond anaphase → impaired nuclear envelope reformation, cytokinesis failure, and cells may re-enter S phase without completing mitosis (endoreduplication)
  • C) Cells would not be able to enter S phase because Cdh1 is required for origin firing
  • D) Cells would arrest permanently in metaphase because Cdh1 controls kinetochore-microtubule attachments
Worked Solution

Answer: B — The APC/C has two activators: Cdc20 (active during mitosis, triggers anaphase) and Cdh1 (active from late mitosis through G1). Their functions differ: Cdc20-APC/C at metaphase → anaphase transition degrades securin (releasing separase → cohesin cleavage → anaphase) and begins cyclin B degradation. Cdh1-APC/C is critical for: completing cyclin B destruction in late mitosis/G1 (maintaining low CDK1 activity), degrading geminin (an S phase inhibitor), stabilising G1 by keeping CDK1/2 activity low. Without Cdh1: cyclin B persists → CDK1 remains active → G1 shortened or absent; cells may undergo endoreduplication (re-replicate DNA without completing mitosis) as CDK1 cannot be fully inactivated. Also, Cdh1 maintains genome stability by degrading proteins like Polo-like kinase (Plk1) and aurora kinase B after mitosis. Cdh1 is a tumour suppressor — loss is associated with cancer.

Hard

Chiasmata are the physical manifestation of crossing-over visible under the microscope during meiosis I. What would happen to chromosome segregation if no chiasmata formed between a pair of homologs?

  • A) The homologs would segregate perfectly, as chiasmata are not needed for segregation in mammals
  • B) Without chiasmata, homologs are not physically connected at metaphase I (they only pair loosely) → tension between kinetochores cannot be properly established → spindle assembly checkpoint may not be satisfied → homologs may segregate randomly (non-disjunction) → aneuploid gametes → Down syndrome, miscarriage, infertility
  • C) The cell would immediately undergo apoptosis when absence of chiasmata is detected
  • D) Meiosis II would compensate by generating additional genetic combinations
Worked Solution

Answer: B — Chiasmata serve dual functions: (1) genetic: generate new allele combinations (crossover products); (2) mechanical: hold homologs together at metaphase I, allowing bipolar tension to develop across the bivalent. At metaphase I, each homolog's kinetochore should be attached to microtubules from ONE pole (monopolar orientation — opposite to metaphase II and mitosis). Chiasmata ensure the bivalent (tetrad) is held together so that kinetochores of homologs can achieve proper bipolar attachment (one homolog to each pole). Without chiasmata: "univalents" form — individual chromosomes that have no partner to hold them; they align randomly at metaphase I and can be lost from the cell or missegregate. This is why obligate crossover (at least one per chromosome arm) is enforced by meiotic recombination machinery. In women, achiasmate (non-crossover) chromosomes are held together by cohesins on chromosome arms, but as women age, cohesins deteriorate → achiasmate bivalents separate prematurely → non-disjunction risk rises → age-related aneuploidy (Down syndrome risk increases with maternal age).

Hard

Stem cells in the intestinal crypt divide rapidly and replenish the intestinal epithelium every ~5 days. Yet intestinal cancer is not universal despite millions of stem cell divisions daily. What cell biology mechanisms protect against transformation of these rapidly dividing cells?

  • A) Intestinal stem cells do not replicate their DNA — they use RNA for division
  • B) Multiple mechanisms: (1) high-fidelity replication (proofreading + MMR); (2) G1/S and G2/M checkpoints monitor DNA integrity; (3) p53 pathway triggers apoptosis if DNA damage exceeds repair capacity; (4) Wnt/Notch/BMP signalling gradients restrict stem cells to the crypt base — differentiated cells are shed from villi tips within 5 days; (5) immunosurveillance — immune cells patrol the epithelium and eliminate nascent cancer cells; (6) clonal competition — cells with mutations that impair fitness are outcompeted by neighbours
  • C) Intestinal stem cells have a special mutation-resistant nucleotide that prevents errors
  • D) The immune system destroys all cells that have divided more than 10 times
Worked Solution

Answer: B — This is an integrative question connecting cell division, DNA repair, checkpoints, and tumour suppression. The colon is indeed one of the most common cancer sites — ~50,000 deaths/year in Australia — reflecting the burden of rapid proliferation. But cancer requires multiple oncogenic mutations that overwhelm all these mechanisms (e.g., APC loss → Wnt activation → KRAS mutation → TP53 loss → SMAD4 loss in colorectal cancer, Fearon-Vogelstein progression model). Normal protection: (1) proofreading makes ~1 error/10⁷ bp/division; (2) checkpoints halt replication at damage; (3) apoptosis eliminates heavily damaged cells; (4) cell shedding means differentiated cells are quickly expelled before accumulating mutations (the crypt-to-villus axis functions as a "conveyor belt"); (5) tumour microenvironment surveillance. Cancer circumvents all of these over decades.

Hard

A diploid organism with 2n=6 undergoes meiosis. How many unique genotypic combinations are possible due to independent assortment alone (ignoring crossing over)?

  • A) 6
  • B) 8
  • C) 2³ = 8 unique gamete genotypes; 4³ = 64 possible zygote genotype combinations from two such gametes (or 8 × 8 = 64)
  • D) 2⁶ = 64 unique gamete genotypes
Worked Solution

Answer: C — For 2n=6: there are n=3 pairs of homologs. For each pair, the gamete receives either the maternal or paternal homolog (2 choices). With 3 independent pairs: 2³ = 8 unique gamete genotypes by independent assortment. When two such gametes combine at fertilisation: 8 × 8 = 64 possible zygote genotype combinations. For humans (n=23): 2²³ ≈ 8.4 million unique gametes, and 2²³ × 2²³ ≈ 70 trillion possible zygotes — before crossing over. This astronomically large number of possible genetic combinations is why no two humans (except identical twins from the same zygote) have ever been or will ever be genetically identical. Option D (2⁶ = 64) would be wrong — for 2n=6, there are 3 chromosome PAIRS (n=3), so 2³ gamete types, not 2⁶.

Hard

Parthenogenesis (egg development without fertilisation) has been reported in some normally sexual species (e.g., Komodo dragons). What genetic consequences does parthenogenesis have compared to sexual reproduction?

  • A) Parthenogenetic offspring are genetically identical to the mother in all cases
  • B) Parthenogenetic offspring are NOT identical to the mother if the egg underwent meiosis — crossing over and independent assortment during the mother's meiosis created genetic variation in the egg's haploid genome; but they are more homozygous than sexually-produced offspring (no paternal contribution). Komodo parthenogenesis produces diploid offspring by fusion of two haploid products of meiosis (automixis) — offspring are more homozygous but not clones
  • C) Parthenogenetic offspring are all female because they lack a Y chromosome
  • D) Parthenogenesis produces genetically superior offspring because they avoid passing on paternal genetic defects
Worked Solution

Answer: B — Parthenogenesis types: (1) Apomixis (apomeiotic): egg produced by mitosis, not meiosis → true clone of the mother. (2) Automixis (meiotic): egg produced by meiosis → genetic variation from crossing over/independent assortment; diploidy restored by various mechanisms (e.g., fusion of egg + second polar body = terminal fusion → increases homozygosity at loci distal to crossovers; fusion of two secondary oocytes = central fusion → increases homozygosity less). Komodo dragons use terminal fusion → highly homozygous but not identical to mother. In species with ZW sex determination (Komodo: ZZ=male, ZW=female): terminal fusion of WW eggs → WW offspring (may not be viable) and ZW (female) offspring. Only ZW survive → sex ratio skewed toward females (not all female as option C states). Option C is wrong because in ZW species, parthenogenetic offspring can be male (ZZ) or female (ZW) depending on the mechanism.
